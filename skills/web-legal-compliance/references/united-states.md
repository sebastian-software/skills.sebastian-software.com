# United States

Use this route when the operator, affected people, transactions, marketing, or
regulated activity connects to the United States. Do not start from a presumed
federal `Impressum` requirement. Map federal, state, local, and sector rules to
the service's actual activity.

## Research Order

1. Identify the operator's formation, principal place of business, licences,
   and regulated status.
2. Identify intentionally targeted states and where customers or affected
   people reside.
3. Record scale and threshold facts without assuming they are current:
   revenue, volumes, data use, data sales or sharing, audience age, entity
   type, and statutory exemptions.
4. Research each relevant issue through current statutes, regulations,
   regulator guidance, and controlling decisions.

## Issue Map

### Operator identity and commerce

There is no single general federal legal-notice page equivalent to the DACH
`Impressum`. Check entity, assumed-name, licensing, professional, consumer,
marketplace, invoice, and sector rules for the specific state and transaction.
Publish accurate identity and contact information where required or useful,
but label trust content as a recommendation rather than federal law.

For sales and subscriptions, investigate price presentation, material terms,
returns, delivery, automatic renewal, free-to-paid conversion, cancellation,
reviews, rankings, and state consumer-protection rules. Review the full flow,
not only Terms of Service.

### Privacy

Build a state applicability matrix from current official sources. Verify each
law's thresholds, exemptions, covered data and people, notice content, rights,
appeals, sensitive-data rules, sale/share or targeted-advertising definitions,
processor duties, universal opt-out signals, and effective dates.

Detect recognized universal opt-out mechanisms at both the request and browser
layers when the applicable law or approved policy requires them. Map the signal
to the rights actually covered in that jurisdiction, such as sale, sharing, or
targeted advertising. Do not describe GPC as a universal analytics opt-out, and
do not let a stored preference silently override a binding signal without
source-backed Product/Legal approval.

Do not use California as a proxy for every state. Check California statutes,
regulations, the California Privacy Protection Agency, and Attorney General
guidance when California applies, then research every other connected state
independently. Add federal and state child, student, health, biometric,
financial, communications, and video privacy regimes when the facts trigger
them.

Read [California](united-states-california.md) whenever California residents,
transactions, tracking, formation, or business activity may be in scope. Do
not use the CCPA threshold analysis as a shortcut for CalOPPA, automatic
renewal, direct-marketing, data-broker, or communications-privacy rules.

### Advertising and communications

- Apply FTC Act truthfulness and unfairness principles to representations,
  including privacy and security promises.
- Make material connections, endorsements, native advertising, rankings, and
  affiliate relationships clear where the consumer encounters the claim; a
  remote legal page may be insufficient.
- For commercial email, verify CAN-SPAM header, subject, identification,
  physical-address, opt-out, and operational requirements.
- For calls and texts, research the TCPA, FCC rules, state mini-TCPA laws, and
  consent evidence. Do not infer SMS compliance from an email checkbox.

### Specialized services

Investigate separate rules for children, online marketplaces, health,
financial services, education, employment, housing, credit, alcohol, gambling,
and other regulated sectors. For accessibility, establish the entity, service,
jurisdiction, current legal authority, and risk with qualified counsel; route
technical remediation to `effective-web`.

## Starting Sources

Open and verify current statutes, regulations, and linked guidance:

- [Federal Trade Commission business guidance](https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance)
- [FTC advertising and marketing basics](https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/advertising-marketing)
- [FTC privacy and security guidance](https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/privacy-security)
- [FTC CAN-SPAM compliance guide](https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/resources/can-spam-act-compliance-guide-business)
- [FTC Children's Online Privacy Protection Rule](https://www.ftc.gov/legal-library/browse/rules/childrens-online-privacy-protection-rule-coppa)
- [Colorado Attorney General: Colorado Privacy Act](https://coag.gov/resources/colorado-privacy-act/)
- [Congress.gov](https://www.congress.gov/)
- [GovInfo](https://www.govinfo.gov/)
- [USA.gov state and territory governments](https://www.usa.gov/state-governments)

Use state legislature, Attorney General, and specialist regulator sites for
each state. A tracker or law-firm summary may help discover changes but is not
the final authority.
