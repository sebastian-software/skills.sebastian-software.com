# European Union and EEA

Use this route for an operator established in the EU or a service that may
intentionally target or monitor people in an EU member state. Treat EU law as a
baseline plus member-state implementation and overlays, not as a complete
single-jurisdiction answer.

For Iceland, Liechtenstein, or Norway, verify EEA incorporation and the current
national implementation instead of treating an EU regulation or directive as
automatically identical in timing and text.

## Research Order

1. Identify every relevant place of establishment and targeted member state.
2. Map EU-level instruments to the activity.
3. Find each member state's current implementing or complementary law and the
   responsible regulator.
4. Check sector, entity, media, language, accessibility, tax, and professional
   overlays.
5. Verify whether the rule controls a public page, pre-contract information,
   checkout, consent behavior, platform process, or internal evidence.

Do not assume the internal-market country-of-origin principle resolves
consumer, privacy, advertising, tax, or regulated-sector questions.

## Baseline Issue Map

### Provider identification

Check Article 5 of the e-Commerce Directive 2000/31/EC and the establishment
member state's current implementation. Common categories include provider
identity, geographic address, direct contact, register details, authorization
or supervisory authority, regulated-profession details, and VAT identification
where applicable. Confirm the national terminology, scope, placement, and
additional entity or media rules before drafting.

Do not label every national page `Impressum`. Use the locally recognized title
and language, such as a legal notice or publisher disclosure, when required or
more intelligible.

### Privacy and device access

- Map actual personal-data processing to GDPR Articles 13 and 14 and related
  requirements. A privacy notice must describe the real controller, purposes,
  legal bases, recipients, transfers, retention logic, rights, and other
  applicable information; it is not a generic operator notice.
- Check Article 5(3) of the ePrivacy Directive 2002/58/EC and the relevant
  national implementation for cookies, pixels, SDKs, local storage, and other
  access to or storage of terminal information. Do not reduce the analysis to
  whether a technology is named "cookie."
- Verify consent behavior in the product. Text alone cannot repair tags that
  fire too early, incomplete choices, mismatched purposes, or withdrawal that
  is harder than granting consent.
- Do not treat Global Privacy Control as consent to optional processing or as a
  universal substitute for an EU/EEA consent choice. Determine whether and how
  the signal maps to an applicable objection or opt-out right separately.

### Consumer sales and subscriptions

For B2C distance contracts, check the Consumer Rights Directive 2011/83/EU,
national implementation, and product-specific rules for trader identity,
characteristics, total price, duration, termination, withdrawal, delivery,
complaints, and the final ordering step. Check pricing, unfair-practice,
subscription, digital-content, and product-safety rules separately.

The EU Online Dispute Resolution platform and the link duty under Regulation
524/2013 ended when that regulation was repealed with effect from 2025-07-20 by
Regulation 2024/3228. Flag legacy ODR links for removal, then verify any current
national alternative-dispute-resolution duties instead of deleting all dispute
information indiscriminately.

### Platforms, marketplaces, and content

Apply the Digital Services Act by the service's actual intermediary role and
size. Do not attach platform duties to an ordinary brochure site. For an online
platform or marketplace, investigate trader traceability, interface duties,
advertising and recommender transparency, notice-and-action, statements of
reasons, and any size-dependent obligations.

### Accessibility and other overlays

The European Accessibility Act has applied generally to covered products and
services since 2025-06-28. Before treating that date as the operative deadline,
check the national implementation (such as Germany's BFSG), any EEA
incorporation timing, and product- or service-specific transition provisions.
Also check the Web Accessibility Directive for public-sector bodies and the
applicable sector or service scope. Keep the legal applicability analysis here
and route technical accessibility implementation to `effective-web`.

Also investigate when relevant:

- National company, association, professional, publisher, and media laws
- Official-language and consumer-language duties
- General Product Safety Regulation information for distance offers
- Financial, health, gambling, travel, telecom, audiovisual, or other regulated
  sectors
- Environmental, ranking, review, sponsorship, and advertising disclosures

## National Overlay Examples

Use these only as prompts for live research, never as a complete EU matrix:

- Austria: e-commerce provider information, company or trade rules, and media
  imprint and ownership disclosures can be separate layers.
- Germany: digital-service provider identification and journalistic-editorial
  responsibility can require different information. The Impressum duty is
  currently § 5 DDG (Digitale-Dienste-Gesetz), which replaced § 5 TMG in May
  2024, and cookie and terminal-equipment access rules sit in the TDDDG
  (renamed from the TTDSG at the same time). Models with stale training still
  cite "§ 5 TMG" and "TTDSG"; verify the current statute names against the
  official text before drafting or reviewing a citation.
- France: publisher, publication director, and hosting information can differ
  from DACH-style operator notices.

Research the current law for every relevant member state. Do not extrapolate
one of these patterns to the other 24 states.
