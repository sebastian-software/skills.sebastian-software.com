# California

Use this route whenever California residents, consumers, customers,
transactions, tracking, subscriptions, or business activity may be in scope.
California has several independent regimes; do not reduce the analysis to
whether the CCPA applies.

## Applicability Order

1. Identify the operator, entity type, California business activity, affected
   people, transaction, data flows, and service providers or third parties.
2. Test each statute independently. Record its own definition, threshold,
   exemption, effective date, and territorial connection.
3. Check the current statute, CPPA regulations, Attorney General guidance, and
   controlling case law. California privacy rulemaking and litigation evolve
   too quickly for remembered summaries to be the final authority.
4. Separate public-page content from notices at collection, preference signals,
   request operations, contracts, retention, cancellation, and internal
   evidence.

A company can fall outside the CCPA business thresholds and still be covered
by CalOPPA or an automatic-renewal rule. Conversely, a California visitor does
not by itself prove that every California statute applies.

## Privacy Surfaces

### CalOPPA

Check Business and Professions Code sections 22575–22579 for a commercial
website or online service that collects personally identifiable information
online about California-resident consumers. When it applies, verify a
conspicuously available privacy policy and the statute's required disclosures,
including collected-information and third-party categories, the change
process, effective date, and response to browser do-not-track or other choice
mechanisms.

Do not omit a privacy policy merely because the operator is below the CCPA
threshold. Do not describe CalOPPA's do-not-track disclosure as a universal
requirement to honor every signal; analyze the separate CCPA/CPRA and GPC rules.

### CCPA and CPRA amendments

First verify the current definition of a covered business, inflation-adjusted
revenue threshold, data-volume and revenue tests, controlled entities,
exemptions, and the affected consumer and data categories.

For a covered business, map at least:

- Notice at or before collection, including categories, purposes, sale or
  sharing, sensitive information, and retention period or criteria
- Online privacy-policy and California-rights content, request methods, and the
  required review cadence
- Access, deletion, correction, portability, limitation, opt-out, and other
  currently applicable request operations
- Sale, sharing, cross-context behavioral advertising, service-provider and
  contractor relationships based on real data flows and contracts
- Financial-incentive or price-difference notices when value is exchanged for
  personal information
- Current CPPA rules for risk assessments, cybersecurity audits, automated
  decisionmaking, or other operational duties when their triggers apply

Do not call every analytics or advertising integration a sale or sharing. Do
not accept a vendor label such as `processor` or `service provider` without
checking the contract and actual use.

### Opt-out links, sensitive information, and GPC

If the business sells or shares personal information, or uses or discloses
sensitive personal information beyond permitted purposes, determine which
homepage links, combined choice surface, and privacy-policy statements current
law requires. Implement opt-out preference signals according to current CCPA
and CPPA rules.

- Detect the recognized signal at the request and browser layers where needed.
- Apply it to covered sale, sharing, and sensitive-information behavior; do not
  describe it as a universal first-party analytics opt-out.
- Do not require an account or unnecessary identifying data for an opt-out.
- Keep tag managers, vendors, server events, and the preference center aligned.
- Test conflicts between GPC and stored choices without assuming that a prior
  `Accept all` wins.

For consumers under 16, separately verify the opt-in rules for sale or sharing
and every child- or teen-specific law triggered by the service.

## Commerce and Marketing

### Automatic renewals

For an automatic-renewal or continuous-service offer to a California consumer,
check Business and Professions Code sections 17600–17606. Review the entire
flow for clear proximate terms, affirmative consent and its retained evidence,
acknowledgment, trial or promotional conversion, renewal reminders, fee and
material-change notices, and cancellation in the required medium. For online
acceptance, verify that online termination is direct and does not obstruct or
delay cancellation.

The amendments applying to relevant contracts entered into, amended, or
extended from 2025-07-01 added material requirements. Verify the current text
and contract date instead of applying a legacy checkout checklist.

### Direct marketing and data brokers

- Check Civil Code section 1798.83, commonly called `Shine the Light`, when a
  business with California customers discloses specified personal information
  to third parties for their own direct marketing. Verify exemptions, request
  channels, response duties, and any `Your California Privacy Rights` surface.
- Check the Delete Act, annual registration, DROP, deletion-processing,
  reporting, and audit duties when the operator meets the current data-broker
  definition. Do not apply data-broker duties to an ordinary first-party
  business without testing the definition.

## Counsel-Required Risk Areas

Route current California counsel into the review when the site uses session
replay, chat, call recording, pixels, tracking URLs, or similar technology that
could implicate the California Invasion of Privacy Act or other
communications-interception theories. Statutory wording and rapidly changing
case law do not support a universal `cookie banner fixes CIPA` conclusion.

Also research current California rules and litigation for minors, health or
biometric data, accessibility under the Unruh Civil Rights Act, pricing,
reviews, loyalty programs, and regulated sectors when the facts trigger them.
