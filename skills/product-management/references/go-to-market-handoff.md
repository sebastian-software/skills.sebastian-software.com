# Go-to-Market Handoff

Carry product evidence into positioning, pricing, launch, sales, and channels.
Do not make go-to-market teams rediscover the customer or invent certainty that
the product team does not have.

## Prepare the evidence packet

Hand off:

- target user, triggering situation, struggle, and desired progress
- current alternatives and the reason a customer changes now
- product mechanism and time to first and repeated value
- observed customer language, proof, objections, anxieties, and switching costs
- buyer, user, approver, blocker, and implementation roles
- supported use cases, non-goals, quality bar, and operational constraints
- willingness-to-pay evidence and relevant economics
- known unknowns, confidence, and dated source context

Keep verbatim research distinct from the team's interpretation. Do not turn an
unvalidated product thesis into a marketing fact.

## Align promise and product

For every major claim, identify:

| Claim | Product evidence | Customer proof | Constraint |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Outcome | Capability and observed path | Result or commitment | Segment and context |
| Speed | Measured time to value | Representative example | Setup and dependencies |
| Ease | Usability evidence | Adoption behavior | Required expertise |
| Difference | Mechanism and alternative | Switching reason | Where it is not better |

Remove or qualify claims the shipped experience cannot reliably support.

## Treat distribution as a product constraint

Check whether the intended channel can reach the segment at sustainable cost
and whether the product supports that channel's trust and sales requirements.
High price, integration, risk, and buyer complexity usually demand more human
support. A low-price self-service product needs a correspondingly clear promise,
fast onboarding, and low support burden.

Do not assume a good product markets itself. Product quality can strengthen
retention and referral, but it does not create awareness or a repeatable channel
by itself.

## Route specialized work

- Use external `customer-research` for a dedicated research program.
- Use external `product-marketing` for positioning and messaging artifacts.
- Use external `pricing` for pricing research and packaging decisions.
- Use external `marketing-plan` and `launch` for channel and launch execution.
- Use `linkedin-social-selling` only when LinkedIn fits the target buyer and
  touch model.
- Use `effective-web` for the implemented web journey and measurement surfaces.
- Use `web-legal-compliance` before tracking, testimonials, lead capture, or
  direct-marketing behavior is approved.

Keep market benchmarks, platform tactics, launch calendars, and channel advice
time-bound and source-backed. Treat simple pricing multipliers and exposure
counts as test hypotheses, not universal laws.

## Quality Check

- Can every claim be traced to product behavior or real customer evidence?
- Are audience, buying context, objections, and alternatives specific?
- Do price, sales effort, onboarding, and support economics agree?
- Is distribution included in feasibility and scope decisions?
- Are volatile tactics clearly dated and verified?
- Can post-launch evidence flow back into the product decision?
