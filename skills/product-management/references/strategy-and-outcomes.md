# Strategy and Outcomes

Strategy is a set of connected choices about whom to serve, what progress to
enable, how the product will win, and what the team will deliberately not do.

## Write the product thesis

Use an evidence-backed statement:

> For [specific user] in [triggering situation], the product enables [valuable
> progress] by [credible mechanism], unlike [current alternative], while
> supporting [business result and constraint].

Label weak parts as hypotheses. Do not turn a positioning sentence into proof.

## Connect user and business outcomes

Separate the levels:

- **User outcome:** the changed state the user values.
- **Behavioral signal:** evidence that the product is enabling that change.
- **Product output:** the capability or intervention the team ships.
- **Business outcome:** sustainable revenue, retention, efficiency, risk
  reduction, or strategic advantage.
- **Guardrail:** trust, safety, accessibility, privacy, reliability, margin, or
  another condition that must not be traded away.

Avoid goals such as “ship feature X” unless shipping itself is the decision.
Name the behavior or result the feature is expected to change.

## Choose an initial wedge

Favor a target situation where the team has several advantages:

- direct access and domain understanding
- a consequential and recurring struggle
- an identifiable trigger for action
- a buyer who can decide and pay
- a narrow outcome that can be delivered well
- a plausible path to repeated value
- distribution the team can actually operate

Product-market fit and founder-market fit are separate questions. A profitable
market can still be wrong for a team that lacks access, conviction, capability,
or values alignment.

## Account for the touch model

Sales and delivery motion follow economics and risk more than the label
“product” or “service.” Higher price, complexity, integration, buyer count, and
switching risk usually require more human trust and support. Lower-touch models
require clearer positioning, faster time to value, and stronger self-service.

State the intended model:

- price and value hypothesis
- acquisition and sales effort
- implementation and support burden
- expected time to first and repeated value
- retention or repeat-purchase mechanism
- gross-margin or capacity constraint

Do not design an enterprise product with consumer onboarding assumptions, or a
low-price product that requires bespoke sales and delivery.

## Make the choices explicit

For each strategic decision, record:

1. context and evidence
2. options considered
3. chosen direction and why
4. non-goals and excluded segments
5. constraints and dependencies
6. leading and lagging signals
7. review date and conditions that trigger reconsideration

Keep a strategy stable long enough to learn, but do not protect it from contrary
evidence because of sunk cost.

When the decision is durable, cross-cutting, or costly to reverse, record it in
the repository's ADR convention. Use the `decision-records` skill to preserve
the rationale, alternatives, consequences, and review triggers rather than
creating a parallel strategy artifact.

## Use a coherent portfolio

When the company has multiple products or services, prefer shared customer,
problem, proof, channel, and trust where possible. A coherent portfolio can
offer different depths or delivery models for the same audience. Unrelated
offers multiply acquisition, support, and learning costs.

## Quality Check

- Does the thesis name a user, situation, progress, mechanism, and alternative?
- Are outputs separated from user and business outcomes?
- Is distribution treated as a design constraint rather than a post-build task?
- Do the touch model and unit economics agree?
- Are non-goals strong enough to guide scope and prioritization?
- Is there a dated decision rule for changing course?
