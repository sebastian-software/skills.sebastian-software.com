# Scope and Prioritization

Use scope to make a product coherent and learnable. Do not turn prioritization
into a ranking contest detached from strategy, evidence, and capacity.

## Define the smallest coherent product

Start with one target user, triggering situation, and job. Include the complete
path needed to reach a trustworthy first result:

```text
enter -> understand -> act -> receive feedback -> recover -> complete -> return
```

Remove adjacent personas, optional workflows, automation, configuration, and
scale work unless they are necessary for the core promise. “Minimum” means the
smallest scope that can test value honestly, not the lowest acceptable quality.

## Evaluate feature requests

Translate a request into evidence before choosing a solution:

- Who experienced the problem, in what situation?
- What outcome were they blocked from reaching?
- How often and how severely does it occur?
- What workaround or alternative do they use now?
- Which segment and strategic choice does it support?
- Is there adoption, retention, revenue, risk, or support evidence?
- What is the smallest intervention, including non-product changes?
- What commitment supports the claim that the requester will use or pay for it?

Do not promise work merely because a prominent customer, loud stakeholder, or
large number of survey respondents asks for it.

## Prioritize decisions, not scores

Use a scoring method only as a conversation aid. Consider:

- expected user and business outcome
- evidence strength and uncertainty reduced
- strategic and segment fit
- reach and frequency in the target situation
- trust, safety, compliance, and operational risk
- time, complexity, reversibility, and opportunity cost
- effect on product clarity and long-term maintenance

Show the inputs and uncertainty. A precise score built from invented numbers is
less useful than a transparent tradeoff.

## Set a contextual quality tier

Define acceptance before implementation:

- core outcome and correctness
- clarity and first-use comprehension
- reliability, recovery, and data integrity
- accessibility, performance, privacy, and security
- supported platforms, states, and scale
- visual and interaction consistency
- support, observability, rollback, and ownership

Raise the bar for irreversible, high-risk, high-frequency, trust-sensitive, or
brand-defining work. For reversible experiments, keep scope small while
preserving the stated guardrails.

## Build an outcome roadmap

Organize the roadmap by outcomes, risks, or learning horizons rather than a
long list of promised features. For each item include:

- target outcome and segment
- evidence and unresolved assumption
- candidate interventions, not a fixed solution when discovery is open
- owner and capacity boundary
- decision or learning checkpoint
- dependencies and explicit non-goals

Use now/next/later or another low-precision horizon unless delivery commitments
require dates. Distinguish a contractual commitment from an internal forecast.

## Negotiate scope constructively

When the request exceeds time or capacity, do not answer with an unexplained
no. Offer a smaller outcome, reduced audience, manual step, staged release, or
later decision. Make the consequence visible:

> We cannot deliver all of [scope] at [quality] by [date]. We can protect
> [critical outcome] by removing [items], or keep the scope and move [date].

## Quality Check

- Does the initial scope complete one real job end to end?
- Are feature requests traced to situations and outcomes?
- Are scoring assumptions visible and challengeable?
- Does the quality tier match risk and reversibility?
- Are non-goals and opportunity costs explicit?
- Can stakeholders tell forecasts, experiments, and commitments apart?
