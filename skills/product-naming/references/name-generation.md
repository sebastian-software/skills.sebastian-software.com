# Name Generation

Generate from the naming brief, not from arbitrary wordplay. A useful territory
connects the product promise to a distinct family of names without predetermining
one answer.

## Choose relevant territories

Consider:

- **Outcome:** the progress or changed state the customer wants
- **Mechanism:** how the product creates that progress
- **Metaphor:** a concrete system, place, craft, motion, or relationship
- **Character:** how the product should feel when used
- **Origin:** a real linguistic, geographic, or domain-specific root
- **Invented form:** a pronounceable construction with an explainable source

Use only territories supported by the product. A security product does not need
fortress imagery if its advantage is simple recovery; a collaboration product
does not need another generic variation on connect or sync.

## Vary the structures

Explore several forms:

- familiar word used distinctively
- meaningful compound
- short phrase or two-word construction
- classical or regional root with verified meaning
- coined word with simple phonotactics
- founder, place, or story-based name when authentic

Avoid producing a longlist where every option shares the same suffix or sound.
Do not force Latin, Greek, German, or another language merely to create a claim
of sophistication or European character.

## Design for speech and memory

Check:

- likely pronunciation in every required language
- number of plausible spellings after hearing it once
- stress pattern and syllable rhythm
- awkward consonant clusters or vowel ambiguity
- resemblance to common category terms or competitors
- ease of saying it in a sentence, email address, and phone call
- recall after a distraction or short delay

Write a simple pronunciation guide when needed. IPA can supplement the guide,
but should not be the only explanation for a general audience.

## Run the creative pass

1. Generate roughly 10–15 raw names per territory.
2. Remove duplicates and near-duplicates before discussion.
3. Annotate intended origin and association without availability claims.
4. Apply the hard constraints and obvious linguistic checks.
5. Keep a varied longlist for the evidence-backed screening pass.

Do not fall in love with the first fluent list. Model-generated names tend to
cluster around familiar technology patterns; a second pass should explicitly
seek different structures, sounds, and emotional character.

## Fast rejection rules

Reject or strongly penalize a candidate when it:

- is difficult to hear, spell, or distinguish in a required language
- contains an unintended negative, embarrassing, or exclusionary meaning
- copies the structure or identity of a known competitor
- is a generic description with little source-identifying power
- depends on a false etymology or explanation
- creates a misleading category, geography, or capability claim
- cannot scale to the intended portfolio without confusion

Do not reject a promising name merely because its exact `.com` is unavailable
when the product has other appropriate domains and channels. Treat the domain
strategy as one decision input, not the creative brief itself.
