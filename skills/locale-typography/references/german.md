# German

Support `de-DE`, `de-AT`, `de-CH`, `de-BE`, and `de-LU`.

## Quotation marks

| Locale | Outer | Nested |
| --- | --- | --- |
| `de-DE`, `de-AT`, `de-BE`, `de-LU` | `„Beispiel“` | `‚Beispiel‘` |
| `de-CH` | `«Beispiel»` | `‹Beispiel›` |

Place a punctuation mark inside the quotation marks when it belongs to the
quoted text and outside when it belongs to the surrounding sentence.

## Punctuation and characters

- Use an en dash with spaces for a parenthetical interruption: `Das Ergebnis –
  trotz der Verzögerung – überzeugt.`
- Use an en dash without spaces for a compact range when the house style does
  so: `2024–2026`. Do not combine a range dash with `von … bis`.
- Put no space before `, . : ; ? !`.
- Use the typographic apostrophe only where German spelling calls for one; do
  not insert an English possessive apostrophe.
- Use `…` for an ellipsis in typographic prose. Preserve the required space
  when whole words are omitted and no space when a word is interrupted.
- Keep number-unit, number-currency, percent, and section-sign combinations
  together with a non-breaking space where the output format permits it.

## Regional differences

- In Swiss Standard German, write `ss` instead of `ß`, including words such as
  `Strasse`. Do not apply that substitution to names or exact quotations.
- In Germany and Austria, preserve `ß`; use `ẞ` in uppercase text when the
  typeface and house style support it.
- Preserve Austrian vocabulary and administrative terms. Typography does not
  authorize converting them to German usage.
- For `de-BE` and `de-LU`, apply German punctuation but use the full locale for
  regional date, number, and currency presentation.

## Hyphenation

Use current dictionaries and automatic hyphenation with the correct language
tag. Do not revive historical rules such as rewriting `ck` to `k-k`, and do not
encode claims that `st` must remain inseparable. Never alter spelling merely to
force a line break.
