# Research and Synthesis

Use qualitative research to replace important assumptions with situated
evidence. Keep the plan light enough to use, the sessions safe enough for
participants, and the synthesis traceable enough for a team to challenge.

## Plan around decisions

Write a short research plan before recruiting:

1. **Decision and context:** what product or design choice this work should
   inform and what is already believed.
2. **Learning goals:** the behaviors, motivations, environments, workarounds,
   expectations, or constraints that remain unclear.
3. **Participants:** relevant experience and selection criteria, including
   perspectives that the existing team or evidence underrepresents.
4. **Method:** interview, task observation, contextual inquiry, diary work,
   artifact review, desk research, or a deliberate combination.
5. **Ethics and operations:** incentive, consent, recording, data access,
   retention, deletion, accessibility, scheduling, and participant support.
6. **Outputs:** the observations and synthesis artifacts needed for the next
   decision, not a promise that research will validate a preferred solution.

Recruit for relevance and meaningful variation, not demographic box-ticking or
statistical representativeness that a small qualitative sample cannot provide.
Use a short screener to select participants without revealing the answer the
team hopes to hear. Pay strangers for their time and never treat willingness to
participate as permission to record or retain sensitive material.

## Protect participant control

Before a session, explain the purpose, format, duration, recording and data
handling in plain language. Obtain informed consent for recording. Make clear
that participants can skip any question, pause, withdraw, or ask for deletion
without needing to justify the choice.

Match the method and questioning to the sensitivity of the topic. Do not push
for an emotionally revealing story because it would make a compelling finding.
Separate personally identifying information from working observations, limit
access, and carry only the detail needed for the design decision into durable
artifacts.

Test the setup before the first session. Verify audio, recording, permissions,
accessibility, backup notes, and the observer's workspace. A technical failure
must not turn a participant's time into an unusable session.

## Ask about behavior, not approval

Structure a behavioral interview around a few learning areas rather than an
exhaustive questionnaire. For each area, know why the answer could change a
decision.

When the interview must support market, commitment, viability, or product-scope
decisions, apply `product-management` discovery discipline: name the decision,
the uncertainty, the evidence needed, and the resulting commitment. This
reference owns the shared recent-event and neutral-probing baseline below, plus
design-context inquiry and synthesis: behavior, environment, artifacts,
expectations, mental models, and implications for the experience. Use the
optional `product-management` skill when the work also needs its dedicated
product-decision guidance.

- Start with ordinary context and recent routines.
- Ask for specific past events: “Tell me about the last time…”
- Explore triggers, sequence, tools, environment, interruptions, adaptations,
  consequences, and what happened next.
- Ask about current alternatives and why they work or fail in context.
- Use neutral follow-ups such as “What did that look like?” and “Tell me more.”
- Save optional probes so an interesting tangent does not consume the questions
  that every participant needs to encounter.
- End by asking what the interview missed and what the participant wishes a
  designer understood.

Avoid asking whether someone likes a hypothetical feature, would use an
imagined product, or agrees with the team's explanation. Do not convert a
participant's suggested feature directly into a requirement; investigate the
situation and desired outcome behind it.

## Observe action and context

What people remember or say and what they do can diverge. When the decision
depends on sequence, coordination, physical context, tacit work, or tool
switching, observe a real or realistically reconstructed task.

Record discrete observations close to the evidence: action, artifact, quote,
environmental condition, interruption, workaround, or visible result. Label an
interpretation as an interpretation. If one person must facilitate and observe,
prioritize participant safety and conversation live, then review the recording
and make structured observations afterward.

Desk research can add domain language, existing evidence, competing models,
accessibility constraints, and questions worth asking. Record source,
publication context, recency, method, and relevance. Treat it as supporting
evidence rather than a substitute for observing the target situation.

## Use proxy sources without hiding their bias

Reviews, support tickets, community discussions, sales notes, analytics
comments, and public complaints can expose language, failure contexts,
workarounds, and questions worth investigating when direct sessions are
limited. Keep each observation tagged with its source, date, product or plan
context, available participant context, and known selection mechanism.

Name how the channel shapes what appears:

- reviews and public posts often overrepresent unusually strong positive or
  negative experiences;
- support records overrepresent problems people chose and were able to report;
- technical communities often overrepresent expert, skeptical, early-adopter,
  or power-user perspectives;
- sales notes reflect a buying conversation, the recorder's interpretation, and
  incentives that may differ from day-to-day use.

Do not merge these channels into a fictional representative user, count repeated
phrases as prevalence without a defensible denominator, or turn a vivid quote
into a requirement. Use proxy evidence to refine hypotheses, vocabulary,
recruiting, and direct-research questions. State which target perspectives the
available channels cannot reveal.

## Synthesize without laundering assumptions

Use this synthesis chain:

```text
raw material -> observations -> patterns -> interpretation
             -> design principles and opportunities -> solution hypotheses
```

Keep links back to the contributing observation and participant. Affinity work
is one useful method: group related observations, name provisional themes,
split or merge them as understanding changes, and retain important outliers.
There is no ideal number or granularity of themes; a useful theme improves
understanding or supports a decision.

Check representation inside every theme. A large cluster repeated by one vocal
participant is not the same as a pattern across varied participants. Note
contradictions, negative cases, missing perspectives, and where another round
of research would be more honest than a confident generalization.

## Use personas carefully

Create a persona only when a durable behavioral pattern helps the team make
different design decisions. Base it on observed goals, contexts, strategies,
constraints, expectations, and meaningful variations—not a fictional biography
or decorative demographic profile.

Label provisional personas and supplied marketing segments as hypotheses. Keep
the supporting observations and counterexamples accessible, state which
decisions the persona should inform, and retire it when new evidence no longer
supports the pattern.

## Use empathy maps as a situated evidence view

Use an empathy map when the team needs one compact view of what a person says,
does, thinks, feels, notices, or hears in a specific evidenced situation. It is
a lens over observations and cautious interpretation, not a substitute for a
persona, journey, mental model, or research session.

For every entry, retain the source and distinguish observed speech or action
from inferred thought or feeling. Capture contradictions between quadrants
instead of forcing consistency: what someone says can differ from what they do,
and an apparent emotion can have several explanations. Add environment,
accessibility, power, social, and time constraints when they shape the moment.

Name the actor, situation, trigger, and decision the map should inform. Do not
create a generic empathy map for an invented "typical user," decorate it with
unsupported feelings, or treat a full quadrant as evidence of importance. Use
the [evidence-based scenario guidance](#rehearse-evidence-based-scenarios) when
sequence and changing context matter more than a single situated view.

## Rehearse evidence-based scenarios

Use a scenario map when the team needs to reason about a person in a concrete
environment before a solution is fully specified. Treat it as an abstraction of
an evidenced situation, not permission for unconstrained role-play.

Set the scene with the place, timing, surrounding activity, trigger, intended
progress, and important facts from the behavioral pattern. Then document what
the person may say, think, feel, notice, hear, decide, and do across the
situation. Include environmental, social, cognitive, and technical
distractions. Name the real-world motivation that could sustain the activity;
“use the product” is not a motivation.

Keep inferred details plausible and traceable to research. Use contrasting
scenarios to expose how one structure may serve a focused quiet moment but fail
under interruption, shared responsibility, emotional load, or limited time.
Revise or discard a scenario when it becomes a fictional demonstration of the
team's preferred product.

## Research handoff

Deliver:

- decision and learning goals
- sample and known coverage gaps
- method, consent, and data-handling notes
- observation register linked to raw evidence
- themes with supporting and contradicting observations
- behavioral patterns, mental-model clues, and current alternatives
- design principles and opportunity hypotheses
- confidence, limitations, and next research or design decision

Never invent participants, quotes, recordings, observations, or consensus. If
no research exists, provide the plan, script, screener logic, observation
template, and synthesis structure as explicitly provisional material.
