# Process and Environments

Use process to increase confidence at the next decision, not to force creative
work through one universal sequence.

## Treat process as a toolkit

- Name the current uncertainty, blockage, or decision before choosing an
  activity.
- Select the lightest method that can improve shared understanding or expose a
  useful design direction.
- Change fidelity, sequence, and participation to fit the work. A short sketch,
  focused interview, workshop, wireframe, or coded prototype may each be the
  right next move.
- Add structure when coordination, consequence, or ambiguity requires it. Do
  not preserve a ceremony merely because it appears in a standard process.
- Use deadlines and phase boundaries as constraints that enable decisions.
  Design is never exhaustively finished; define what confidence is sufficient
  to move forward.

Scale sensemaking effort with risk. Safety-critical, irreversible, regulated,
or trust-sensitive work needs more evidence and challenge. Reversible product
exploration can move once the largest assumptions and most valuable ideas are
understood well enough to prototype.

## Start with a provisional brief

Capture enough direction to begin without turning assumptions into settled
requirements:

1. **Purpose:** the situation, opportunity, and reason the work might matter.
2. **Expected users:** the people believed to benefit, explicitly marked as
   hypotheses until research supports them.
3. **Design factors:** a small set of qualities or tensions that should guide
   choices, such as expressive versus predictable or focused versus
   explorative.
4. **Scope:** the intended design outcome and current boundary, not a premature
   inventory of screens and features.
5. **Open questions:** what must be learned about people, alternatives, the
   system, constraints, and feasibility.
6. **Evidence plan:** the first activities likely to answer those questions.
7. **Success evidence:** observable task, comprehension, usability, or outcome
   signals rather than completion of design phases.

Return to the brief after research. Replace guesses with evidence, revise the
problem and audience, add explicit non-goals, and turn the provisional project
brief into a decision-grade design brief.

## Separate sensemaking and placemaking

Use **sensemaking** to understand people, problems, systems, objects, rules, and
uncertainty. Research, synthesis, diagrams, system models, sketches, and rough
prototypes all qualify when they answer a question.

Use **placemaking** to arrange content, controls, behavior, style, and movement
so the product communicates purpose and supports the intended activity. Treat
visual character and interaction craft as functional parts of the environment,
not decoration applied after structure.

Move between the two as evidence changes. Do not remain in research because
more questions always exist, and do not polish a place whose purpose and system
remain unclear.

## Decide between a path and an environment

Model every product as an environment containing actors, objects, rules,
states, relationships, and purposes. Then decide how much freedom each moment
should provide.

- Use focused linear paths when order, safety, speed, or a known singular
  outcome matters.
- Use explorable environments when people need to create, organize, compare,
  adapt tools, or solve problems in different valid ways.
- Combine both: provide broad rooms for exploration and focused moments for
  consequential or well-defined tasks.
- Preserve recognizable landmarks, state, navigation, and recovery so autonomy
  does not become disorientation.
- Design causality explicitly. Show how actions, objects, inputs, and state
  changes affect one another.
- Accept purposeful complexity when capability requires it; constrain and teach
  that complexity instead of replacing it with a narrow sequence that removes
  meaningful agency.

## Progress through useful fidelity

Move from broad uncertainty toward concrete experience without jumping directly
to polished screens:

```text
research -> problem model -> solution options -> system and interaction model
         -> structure and rough flows -> wireframes -> realistic prototype
```

Treat this as a map, not a gate sequence. Skip or revisit stages when the next
decision does not need them. At every fidelity, state what question the artifact
answers and what evidence would justify making it more expensive.
