# Autonomy and Motivation

Design engagement around the outcome a person chose, not the product's desire
for repeated activity. Use rewards, progression, prompts, and friction only
when they support agency, growing capability, meaningful connection, or safety.

## Diagnose the motivation context

Before adding an engagement mechanic, document:

- what the person is trying to accomplish outside the product
- whether the activity is chosen, required, habitual, creative, social, or
  safety-critical
- existing intrinsic interest, values, commitments, and social expectations
- capability, time, attention, uncertainty, and environmental constraints
- what successful completion or healthy disengagement looks like
- whose metric improves if the mechanic changes behavior

Do not label a failure to return as low motivation until the team has checked
relevance, effort, accessibility, trust, timing, alternatives, and whether the
product delivered value in the first place.

## Support self-determination

Review the experience through three needs:

- **Autonomy:** meaningful choice, user-defined goals, adaptable organization,
  understandable consequences, consent, and the ability to pause or leave.
- **Competence:** an achievable starting point, clear feedback, progressive
  challenge, recoverable mistakes, and evidence of growing capability.
- **Relatedness:** respectful collaboration, belonging, mutual support, or a
  credible connection between the activity and people who matter.

These are design lenses, not a checklist for manufacturing dependence. A
solitary tool may not require social features; a constrained safety flow may
temporarily limit choice. Explain tradeoffs and keep constraints proportional
to the user's outcome.

Use three supporting checks where the broad needs are too abstract:

- **Fairness:** people receive an equitable experience across disability,
  input mode, language, device, network, status, and other relevant conditions;
  rules and distribution do not quietly privilege a presumed default person.
- **Predictability:** people can recognize available actions, anticipate
  consequences, observe processing, and understand results. Control without a
  usable causal model is not meaningful autonomy.
- **Significance:** the product acknowledges effort and contribution by showing
  their real effect. Recognition should connect an in-product action to an
  outcome that matters, not substitute points or praise for that outcome.

Do not force all six qualities into every product. A short transaction may need
fairness and predictability but no progression or community. State which needs
are relevant to the chosen activity and why.

## Keep rewards informational

Rewards can focus attention and encourage repetition, but they can also replace
the reason someone valued an activity. Prefer feedback that communicates
progress, contribution, mastery, or consequence over arbitrary points and
prizes.

- Tie recognition to an outcome or capability the person understands.
- Let people set, adjust, or decline goals and reminders.
- Keep progress accurate; do not inflate it with meaningless activity.
- Avoid variable rewards whose primary purpose is compulsive checking.
- Do not threaten accumulated status to force return.
- Do not turn rest, cancellation, or a changed goal into failure.
- Watch for crowding out: if an intrinsically meaningful activity becomes a
  chore performed for the reward, remove or redesign the mechanic.

Use celebration sparingly. Match intensity to consequence and frequency,
provide quiet and reduced-motion alternatives, and never let sensory novelty
obscure the resulting state.

## Design progression around capability

Progression should help people understand what is possible and become more
effective, not merely spend longer in the product.

- Establish expectations and the product's core promise early.
- Offer a small successful action with visible cause and effect.
- Introduce complexity when the current goal makes it relevant.
- Keep contextual guidance modular, dismissible, and replayable.
- Preserve expert shortcuts and user-defined structures.
- Make progress portable and durable where lock-in would undermine autonomy.
- Support resumption after absence without shame or artificial recovery work.

Measure meaningful completion, confidence, error recovery, retained
understanding, and reduced unwanted effort. Session count and streak length are
not substitutes for those outcomes.

## Use friction for the person

Friction is justified when it helps someone understand or avoid a material
consequence. Increase it with risk, irreversibility, uncertainty, legal or
financial impact, privacy exposure, or harm to other people.

Useful friction may include a clear review, a consequence preview, explicit
consent, a typed identifier for an irreversible operation, a cooling-off period,
or a safer default. Pair it with explanation and recovery where possible.

Remove friction that exists to exhaust cancellation, hide refusal, encourage
oversharing, obstruct export, manufacture urgency, or turn an accidental tap
into commitment. Apply the same clarity and effort standard to accepting and
declining, joining and leaving, or enabling and disabling.

## Preserve safe exploration

People learn capable products by acting and observing results. Support that
loop with visible state, previews, drafts, history, undo, soft deletion,
reversible defaults, and an anchored way back. Explain why an action failed and
what remains unchanged.

Use focused modes when the person explicitly wants concentration or when a
consequential task benefits from reduced distraction. Make the mode legible and
easy to exit. Do not call an imposed funnel a focus mode when it removes valid
choices for conversion or retention.

## Challenge engagement proposals

Ask:

1. What user-selected outcome does this mechanic improve?
2. Could someone reach that outcome and use the product less?
3. Is the mechanic still defensible if engagement metrics are hidden?
4. What choice, understanding, or recovery does the person retain?
5. Who is excluded, pressured, or harmed by the default?
6. Does it preserve intrinsic interest and healthy stopping points?
7. What evidence would cause the team to remove it?

Reject guilt, disguised advertising, false urgency, streak loss, obstructed
exit, confusing consent, and unpredictable reward loops used to exploit
attention. Recommend a user-aligned alternative and define both benefit and harm
signals for evaluation.
