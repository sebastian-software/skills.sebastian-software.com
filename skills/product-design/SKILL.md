# Product Design

Turn evidence and product intent into an understandable system, a purposeful
environment, and testable experience. Use design artifacts to answer decisions;
do not produce workshops, personas, flows, or polished screens as ceremony.

## Workflow

1. Name the design decision, target situation, current evidence, constraints,
   and artifact needed next. Mark assumptions and supplied personas as such.
2. Write or inspect the provisional brief. Keep audience, problem, design
   factors, scope, open questions, and success evidence distinct from proposed
   features.
3. Select the smallest route that can increase confidence:
   - [Process and environments](references/process-and-environments.md) for
     briefs, process choice, sensemaking, placemaking, autonomy, and fidelity.
   - [Research and synthesis](references/research-and-synthesis.md) for research
     plans, behavioral interviews, observation, desk research, affinity work,
     personas, and empathy maps.
   - [Problem framing](references/problem-framing.md) for problem-space models,
     expectations, mental-model gaps, problem statements, empowerment
     statements, and How Might We questions.
   - [Solution modeling](references/solution-modeling.md) for feature canvases,
     object-oriented UX, object relationships, moments, flows, and journeys.
   - [Structure and prototyping](references/structure-and-prototyping.md) for
     information architecture, thumbnails, wireframes, fidelity choices,
     interactive prototypes, testing, and delivery.
   - [Autonomy and motivation](references/autonomy-and-motivation.md) when the
     experience uses choice, reward, progression, guidance, or engagement
     mechanics.
4. Keep observations, participant language, interpretation, design principles,
   opportunities, and solution ideas in distinguishable layers. Do not turn a
   repeated quote directly into a feature requirement.
5. Explore more than one structural or interaction direction. Compare agency,
   comprehension, consequence, accessibility, implementation constraints, and
   fit with the product outcome.
6. Prototype only the fidelity needed to answer the next question. Test with
   realistic tasks, content, states, and interruptions before increasing craft.
7. Update the design brief with what changed, why, unresolved risks, chosen and
   rejected directions, and the implementation or research handoff.

## Operating Principles

- Treat process as a toolkit. Add an activity because it reduces a named
  uncertainty, not because a diagram says it comes next.
- Design the system before drawing screens. Identify actors, objects, rules,
  relationships, state, permissions, and consequences.
- Separate the problem space from the solution space long enough to understand
  behavior and context, then move between them deliberately as prototypes
  generate new evidence.
- Prefer empowerment framing: understand what people are trying to achieve and
  what helps them exercise agency, not only what is broken or painful.
- Preserve nonlinear exploration where people create, organize, compare, or
  solve problems in different valid ways. Use focused linear paths when order,
  safety, or one known outcome matters.
- Let hierarchy, layout, language, behavior, and visual character communicate
  the purpose of the environment. Craft is part of function.
- Make consequential state visible and actions recoverable. People learn by
  acting and observing results; defensive users do not explore.
- Challenge manipulative motivation and retention mechanics. Choice, reward,
  friction, and guidance must serve the user's goals and autonomy.
- Scale research and testing rigor with risk, reversibility, novelty, and the
  cost of being wrong.
- Never invent participants, observations, quotes, usability findings, or
  evidence. When evidence is absent, deliver a plan, hypothesis, or clearly
  labeled provisional model.

An **evidence register** is the compact trace from a consequential claim to its
source, date, participant or operating context, evidence type, confidence, and
affected decision. Keep observed behavior, measured outcomes, interpretations,
stakeholder claims, and assumptions visibly distinct. Link to raw evidence when
access and consent allow; never turn the register into invented research.

## Default Deliverable

For a broad product-design request, return:

1. Design decision, scope, constraints, and evidence register
2. Target situation, actors, environment, behaviors, and current alternatives
3. System model and important user or team mental-model gaps
4. Problem and empowerment framing with opportunities ordered for design
   exploration
5. Two or more solution directions with tradeoffs and non-goals
6. Chosen object, interaction, navigation, and state model
7. Prototype scope, fidelity, realistic tasks, and evaluation plan
8. Updated design brief, unresolved risks, and handoffs

## Routing Boundaries

- Use `product-management` to decide product strategy, target market, outcomes,
  business viability, roadmap, release scope, and prioritization. Return here to
  shape the experience.
- Use `effective-web` to specify and implement browser UI, visual systems,
  accessibility, responsive behavior, performance, and frontend verification.
- Hand recruitment, fieldwork, and operation of a full customer research
  program to a dedicated research specialist; use this skill to frame design
  questions and synthesize supplied evidence.
- Use `decision-records` when a durable design-system or interaction decision
  needs repository-level rationale and reopening conditions.
