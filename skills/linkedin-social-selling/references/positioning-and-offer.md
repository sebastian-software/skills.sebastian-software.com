# Positioning and Offer

Use this reference before optimizing visibility. More reach only amplifies an
unclear buyer, weak promise, or unproven offer.

## Build the evidence base

1. Select up to three successful or instructive customer projects that the
   business would want to repeat.
2. Record for each project:
   - the human buyer and their role
   - the situation and triggering event
   - the problem in the customer's own words
   - the expert diagnosis, kept separate from that language
   - the desired and achieved outcomes
   - the work, sequence, duration, commercial scope, and constraints
   - the evidence available for public use
   - what made the relationship effective or difficult
3. Compare the cases. Look for a repeatable buyer, trigger, problem, method,
   result, or market context. Do not force a common denominator when the sample
   does not support one.
4. Choose one primary buyer and one situation for the next experiment. Describe
   a person with priorities, incentives, vocabulary, objections, and buying
   context; do not stop at industry and company size.

When no prior customer evidence exists, replace claims with interview and pilot
hypotheses. Do not manufacture a case study.

## Separate entry and core offers

Use a focused entry offer to create a useful first result and reduce the risk of
starting a larger engagement. Keep the core or back-end offer as the longer path
to the complete outcome.

Specify the entry offer with:

- target buyer and triggering situation
- concrete problem and customer language
- format and scope
- tangible output or first result
- duration and participation required
- price or pricing hypothesis
- method and boundary
- proof and qualification criteria
- logical path to the larger offer without hiding the commercial intent

Do not turn the entry offer into free custom consulting or an artificial tripwire.
It must be independently useful and operationally repeatable.

## Write the commercial thesis

Draft a working statement:

> For [specific buyer] facing [recognizable situation], [entry offer] produces
> [tangible first result] in [time or scope], using [credible method or proof],
> and clarifies the path toward [larger outcome].

Treat the statement as a testable thesis. Check it in interviews, messages,
sales calls, and paid work before scaling distribution.

## Turn evidence into sales material

Structure a short offer aid around:

1. recognizable symptoms in customer language
2. the larger problem or cost
3. desired and demonstrated outcomes
4. the path or method
5. format, scope, timing, and price
6. relevant proof
7. next step

Keep the aid scannable and designed to support a conversation. A short list of
the strongest challenges and outcomes is better than an exhaustive deck. Omit
testimonials when permission or evidence is missing.

## Quality Check

- Can the buyer recognize their situation without knowing the expert diagnosis?
- Does the promised result describe a changed state, not merely an activity?
- Is every proof claim traceable to evidence and permission?
- Is the entry offer useful even if the buyer does not purchase the core offer?
- Does the positioning exclude poor-fit work clearly enough to guide decisions?
- Is the next learning step explicit when evidence is weak?
